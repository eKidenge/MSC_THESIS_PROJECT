## Background and Problem Statement
Urban traffic congestion in developing cities such as Nairobi and Nakuru presents significant socio-economic and environmental challenges. Traditional traffic control systems are largely static and inefficient, failing to adapt to real-time, heterogeneous traffic flows involving cars, buses, motorcycles, and matatus.

While Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) offer promising solutions, their deployment in Kenya is constrained by:
- **Data scarcity:** Limited annotated datasets reflecting local vehicle types and driving behaviors.
- **Implementation barriers:** High costs and bureaucratic hurdles associated with sensor deployment and field testing.

---

## Proposed Solution: Simulation-First Approach
This research adopts a simulation-first methodology to overcome real-world deployment challenges. A comprehensive simulation framework will be developed to act as a **digital twin** of a typical Kenyan urban intersection using open-source tools.

The simulated environment will:
- Generate synthetic yet realistic traffic data
- Serve as a controlled testbed for AI-based traffic control
- Enable reproducible, ethical, and cost-effective experimentation

---

## Methodology and Technical Framework
The system integrates multiple AI components within a closed-loop simulation:

1. **Synthetic Data Generation & Modeling**  
   Realistic intersection modeling with Kenyan vehicle types and traffic patterns.

2. **Perception Module**  
   Deep learning models fine-tuned on synthetic data for vehicle detection and tracking.

3. **Prediction Module**  
   Short-term traffic flow forecasting using machine learning techniques.

4. **Adaptive Control & Evaluation**  
   Adaptive traffic signal control strategies evaluated against standard baselines.
